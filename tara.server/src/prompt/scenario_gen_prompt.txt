<Background>
TARA stands for Threat Analysis and Risk Assessment and is the core process of the ISO/SAE 21434 standard, forming the foundation of automotive cybersecurity engineering.

<Damage_Scenario>
A Damage Scenario is the process of identifying potential causes that may compromise the security properties (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) of an asset.

For example, the following Damage Scenarios can be identified for the asset "Data communication (lamp request)":
- Damage Scenario 1 (Integrity, Availability violation): Vehicle cannot be driven at night because the headlamp function is perceived as inhibited while parked.
- Damage Scenario 2 (Integrity violation): Front collision with a narrow stationary object (e.g., a tree) caused by unintended turning-off of the headlamp during night driving at medium speed.

Another example for the asset "Data communication (oncoming car information)":
- Damage Scenario 1 (Integrity violation): Drivers of oncoming vehicles are blinded due to the inability to switch to low beam during night driving.
- Damage Scenario 2 (Availability violation): Malfunctioning automatic high beam caused by the headlamp always remaining at low beam during night driving.
</Damage_Scenario>

<Threat_Scenario>
A Threat Scenario is the process of identifying potential threats to an asset based on the Damage Scenario. It specifically analyzes how the asset can be attacked and what threats may result.

For example:

- Damage Scenario: Front collision with a narrow stationary object caused by unintended turning-off of the headlamp during night driving at medium speed

- Threat Scenario 1: Spoofing of a signal leads to loss of integrity of the “Lamp Request” signal communication to the power switch actuator ECU, potentially causing the headlamp to turn off unintentionally.
- Threat Scenario 2: Tampering with a signal sent by the body control ECU leads to loss of integrity of the “Lamp Request” signal communication to the power switch actuator ECU, potentially causing the headlamp to turn off unintentionally.
</Threat_Scenario>

<Impact_Rating>
The Impact Rating step evaluates the potential negative consequences that the identified Damage Scenarios may have on the system or road users.

Impacts are analyzed across multiple categories to determine severity.

The four primary SFOP categories are:
- Safety
- Financial
- Operational
- Privacy

For example:

- Damage Scenario 1: Vehicle cannot be driven at night because the headlamp function was inhibited while parked. (Impact Category: Operational)
- Damage Scenario 2: Front collision with a narrow stationary object caused by unintended turning-off of the headlamp during night driving. (Impact Category: Safety)
</Impact_Rating>

<Attack_Path_Analysis>
The Attack Path Analysis step examines Threat Scenarios in detail to determine the concrete paths through which the scenario can be realized.
This process identifies how an attacker gains system access and executes the attack.
Each Attack Path MUST include a "via <entry vector>" statement (e.g., via the Bluetooth interface, via the cellular interface).
Recommend an appropriate <via OOO> (entry vector) and detail the step-by-step progression for each path.
The provided shortest path is the backbone. Your job is to expand each backbone step, not to invent a different sequence.

EXAMPLE
Threat scenario: Spoofing of CAN messages for the braking ECU leads to loss of integrity of braking functionality.

Attack path 1:
(1) The telematics ECU is compromised via the cellular interface.
(2) The gateway ECU is compromised via CAN communication from the telematics ECU.
(3) The gateway ECU forwards malicious braking request signals (unwanted rapid deceleration).
(4) Malicious signal spoofing: lamp request (OFF).

Attack path 2:
(1) The attacker compromises the navigation ECU via the Bluetooth interface.
(2) The compromised navigation ECU transmits a malicious control signal.
(3) The gateway ECU forwards the malicious signal to the power switch actuator.
(4) Malicious signal spoofing: lamp request (OFF).

Attack path 3:
(1) The attacker gains local access to the OBD connector.
(2) The attacker sends a malicious control signal through the OBD connector.
(3) The gateway ECU forwards the malicious signal to the power switch actuator.
(4) Malicious signal spoofing: lamp request (OFF).

</Attack_Path_Analysis>
